🔬 Basics of Molecules
What is a molecule? +
A molecule is the smallest unit of a pure substance that retains all of its chemical properties. It consists of two or more atoms held together by chemical bonds (covalent, ionic, or metallic). For example, a water molecule (H₂O) contains two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom bonded covalently.
What is molecular visualisation? +
Molecular visualisation is the representation of molecular structures in 2D or 3D space using diagrams, models, or computer graphics. It helps scientists and students understand the spatial arrangement of atoms, bond angles, lone pairs, and overall geometry — all of which determine how a molecule behaves chemically.
Why do we visualise molecules? +
Visualising molecules allows us to:
  • Predict and understand physical properties (polarity, boiling point, solubility)
  • Understand reaction mechanisms
  • Design drugs and materials at the molecular level
  • Communicate complex structures clearly in research
Without visualisation, it is extremely difficult to reason about three-dimensional chemistry.
What is the difference between molecular formula and molecular structure? +
A molecular formula (e.g. H₂O) tells you only the types and counts of atoms in the molecule. A molecular structure (or structural formula) shows how those atoms are connected and arranged in space — including bond types, lone pairs, and geometry. For example, two molecules can share the formula C₂H₆O but have completely different structures (ethanol vs dimethyl ether).
📐 Molecular Shape
What is molecular shape? +
Molecular shape (also called molecular geometry) describes the three-dimensional arrangement of atoms in a molecule. It is determined by the number of bonding pairs and lone pairs around the central atom, following VSEPR theory. Common shapes include linear, bent, trigonal planar, tetrahedral, and trigonal pyramidal.
Why do molecules have different shapes? +
Molecules have different shapes because the number of electron pairs (both bonding and lone) around the central atom varies. These pairs repel each other (like charges repel) and arrange themselves to be as far apart as possible, creating different geometries. The presence of lone pairs further distorts angles because lone pairs require more space than bonding pairs.
What are bond angles? +
A bond angle is the angle between two adjacent bonds at a central atom. It is measured in degrees and reflects the molecular geometry. For example:
  • Linear (CO₂): 180°
  • Tetrahedral (CH₄): 109.5°
  • Trigonal pyramidal (NH₃): 107°
  • Bent (H₂O): 104.5°
Lone pairs reduce these angles by exerting stronger repulsion than bonding pairs.
What is a linear molecule? +
A linear molecule has all its atoms arranged in a straight line with a 180° bond angle. This occurs when the central atom has exactly 2 bonding regions and no lone pairs. CO₂ and BeCl₂ are classic examples. In CO₂, carbon forms two double bonds with oxygen — the two bonding groups arrange linearly to maximise separation.
⚡ Lone Pairs
What are lone pairs? +
Lone pairs (also called non-bonding pairs) are pairs of valence electrons on an atom that are not involved in forming a chemical bond. They belong exclusively to one atom. For example, oxygen in H₂O has 6 valence electrons — 2 are used for bonding with H and 4 remain as 2 lone pairs.
What is the difference between bond pairs and lone pairs? +
Bond Pairs Lone Pairs
Shared between two atoms Belong to one atom only
Held in place by two nuclei Held by one nucleus only
Occupy less angular space Occupy more angular space
How do lone pairs affect molecular shape? +
Lone pairs occupy more space than bonding pairs because they are only attracted to one nucleus. This greater spatial demand causes them to push bonding pairs closer together, reducing bond angles and changing the molecular shape. A molecule with lone pairs will have a different molecular shape from its electron geometry — for example, NH₃ has tetrahedral electron geometry but trigonal pyramidal molecular shape.
Why do lone pairs reduce bond angles? +
Lone pairs repel bonding pairs more strongly than bonding pairs repel each other. The order of repulsion is:

Lone–Lone > Lone–Bond > Bond–Bond

Because lone pairs exert greater repulsion, they "push" the bonding pairs closer together, reducing the angle between them. Each lone pair on the central atom typically reduces bond angles by approximately 2–2.5°.
Which molecule has more lone pairs: NH₃ or H₂O? +
H₂O has more lone pairs on its central atom (oxygen).

  • H₂O: Oxygen has 6 valence electrons. 2 are used for bonds → 2 lone pairs remain.
  • NH₃: Nitrogen has 5 valence electrons. 3 are used for bonds → 1 lone pair remains.
This is why H₂O has a smaller bond angle (104.5°) than NH₃ (107°) — more lone pairs means greater compression.
How many lone pairs does CH₄ have? +
CH₄ has zero lone pairs on its central carbon atom.

Carbon has 4 valence electrons. All 4 are used to form bonds with the 4 hydrogen atoms. No electrons remain as lone pairs. This is why CH₄ has a perfect tetrahedral geometry with undistorted 109.5° angles — there are no lone pairs to cause compression.
🧲 Shape and Properties
How does molecular shape affect properties? +
Molecular shape determines many key properties:
  • Polarity: Whether bond dipoles cancel (linear = non-polar, bent = polar)
  • Boiling/melting point: Polar molecules have stronger intermolecular forces
  • Solubility: "Like dissolves like" — polar molecules dissolve in polar solvents
  • Reactivity: Shape controls how molecules approach each other in reactions
  • Biological activity: Drug molecules must fit active sites (shape complementarity)
Why is water (H₂O) polar? +
Water is polar for two reasons:

  1. Polar O–H bonds: Oxygen is much more electronegative than hydrogen, so each O–H bond has a partial negative charge (δ⁻) on O and partial positive (δ⁺) on H.
  2. Bent shape: Because H₂O is bent (not linear), the two O–H bond dipoles point in different directions and do not cancel out. They add together to produce a net dipole moment pointing toward oxygen.
This polarity is responsible for water's high surface tension, high boiling point, and ability to act as a universal solvent.
Why is carbon dioxide (CO₂) non-polar despite having polar bonds? +
CO₂ contains two polar C=O bonds (oxygen is more electronegative than carbon), meaning each bond has a dipole moment.

However, CO₂ is linear (180°). The two C=O bond dipoles point in exactly opposite directions and have equal magnitude — they cancel out perfectly, resulting in a net dipole moment of zero.

This makes CO₂ non-polar despite having polar bonds. This is a key example of how molecular shape, not just bond polarity, determines overall molecular polarity.
